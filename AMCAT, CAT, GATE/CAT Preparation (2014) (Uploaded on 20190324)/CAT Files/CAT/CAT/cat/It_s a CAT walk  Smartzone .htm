It’s a CAT walk
Indira Dharchaudhuri

This is no dream, but the unvarnished truth: at a time when phrases like recruitment freeze, economic slowdown and global recession has become de riguer, the average annual salary of Indian Institute of Management (IIM) graduates clocks no less than Rs. 7-7.50 lakhs in India. In fact, some of the best brains bag cushy jobs that bring home more than Rs. 10-11 lakhs per annum. Furthermore, the average annual salary for IIM graduates placed abroad goes above US$ 74,000-1.20 lakhs (nearly Rs. 3.44-5.58 million), or even £ 40,000-60,000! At times, stock options are also thrown in as feel-good sops.

It's no wonder that getting a management diploma from an IIM and, thereby, bagging a job where your salary reads in six figures (or seven, if you're lucky), is a dream worth pursuing. But success, as the adage goes, is 98 per cent perspiration and two per cent inspiration. And this is more so once you consider these statistics: while schools like Wharton or Harvard get around 8,000 applications for nearly 800 seats, IIM Ahmedabad alone gets nearly 40,000 applications for 200 seats. In total, more than 80,000 graduates, including working professionals, take the Common Admission Test (CAT) hoping to make it to some 1,000-odd IIM seats. 

What's hot about CAT?
CAT is conducted by the six IIMs — Ahmedabad, Bangalore, Kolkata, Lucknow, Indore and Kozhikode. Besides these, there are several other institutes like the Fore School of Management, MDI, MICA, Nirma Institute of Management, SP Jain Institute and others, which draw candidates based on their performance in CAT. The exam is scheduled for November 24 this year — with barely seven weeks to go, students also have to consider the GD and interview which they'll be facing after clearing CAT. If they survive this, the next roadblock is IIM, after which, they'll never have to grill themselves to the brink of tolerance, ever again.

However, warns Pavan Chauhan, Centre Manager, Institute of Management Studies (IMS), Delhi, “The entire selection process, by the IIMs, is shrouded in a tremendous amount of secrecy. This uncertainty is further compounded by scores of unanswered questions. Do engineers have an edge over other graduates? Is the academic record an important determinant of success in CAT?
How much weight is attached to work experience?”
Contrary to general perception, CAT, as a test, doesn't only test your memory. Neither does it test your technical expertise in areas like Maths and English. Says Tushar Agarwal, Director, Professional Tutorials (PT), “It's an exam that tests your logic — be it quantitative or verbal. It also tests your time management skills, speed and accuracy, and of course, your mental toughness.”
Competing with fellow aspirants, with a theoretical chance of success of less than two per cent, is an intimidating proposition. Says Chauhan, “Those who choose not to be scared, increase their odds tremendously.”

Though the exam procedure has outlined that questions will be spread over four sections of Quantitative Aptitude (QA), Data Interpretation (DI), Reading Comprehension (RC) and Verbal Ability (VA), the actual paper need not have any pre-set pattern or division of sections. Two hours is given for each section, so speed is important. Says Dr. Sunil Vaid, Head, Delhi Public College of Competition (DPCC), “To make it to the IIMs, a minimum of 20-25 points is considered 'necessary' in each section. An overall score of 120 points (out of 185 or 195) is usually good. However, in an easier paper, 130 is a better bet.” Adds Shonali Gupta, a First Year student of IIM Calcutta (IIM-C), “Guessing a wrong answer can cost points, as the examiners might deduct one-fourth of a point for every wrong answer.” That translates to one lost point for four wrong answers.

Preparation strategy
You have to be thorough with questions on verbal logic and reasoning. “In all likelihood, most questions will be jumbled sentences, syllogisms, critical reasoning (short paragraphs + questions) and to a certain extent, grammar,” says Ulhas Vairgkar, Director, TIME. Elimination is of paramount importance. “Students can hone their skills by using Venn diagrams as reference points,” asserts Satya Narayanan R., Chairman, Career Launcher (CL).

The QA section does not merely test your skill in mathematics. “It's more a test of logic and common sense,” says Hirdesh Madan, Director, Bulls-eye, Chandigarh. So, make it a point to go through all the questions and pick out the easiest ones first. “You can always tackle a difficult question later. But you might find it tiring to come back to a simple question after a difficult one,” says Moheet Gupta, Director, Millennium-The Academy, Kolkata. “Students can practice from the analytical reasoning sections of the GRE test,” adds Narayanan R.

For the DI section, some amount of comfort level with ratios and percentages is a must and scanning is again the key formula. “Normally, questions on pie-charts are comparatively easier to tackle,” says Chauhan.

Coaching helps
“Last minute tips from coaching institutes make a difference,” says Eaishvarya Prakash, a student of IIM Ahmedabad, who took coaching from PT. “I took over 35 mock tests for CAT, which helped me analyse my performance.” Suggests Prateek Roongta, ex-student of PT and an IIM Ahmedabad alumni, currently working with A T Kearney, “It's preferable if you can join some institute that gives you an all India ranking. You'll come to know how good is your preparation."

52 days — a time table
Divide your schedule into two broad categories — new issues to tackle and old topics to revise. In each part, keep slots for English and Maths. Says Archana Jagannathan, a First Year student from IIM-C, "Vocabulary for CAT can be built in 30-40 days and a reading speed of 400-500 words per minute with 80-90 per cent comprehension can be acquired at the same time. In Maths, one must learn the three basic tables, multiplication, inverse, squares and cubes, up to 30."

Start early. "Between October 2-14, clarify all basic confusions and problems regarding fundamental issues," says Narayanan R, adding, "between October 15-27, start taking your first, full practice tests." Actually, "Work on test taking techniques from the beginning," comments Devendra Rawat, a Second Year student of IIM-C. Try to complete the basic syllabus a little before mid-November. As you draw close to the end of October, start identifying your strengths and weaknesses. Understand and decide the order in which to attempt various sections of the test. "Between October 28-November 9, you should be mixing an hour of test-taking session with two hours of analysing. And finally, from November 10-22, freeze everything and concentrate on just taking the tests," advises Dr. Vaid. However, don't forget to have a minimum of 12 hours of sleep on the night of November 23.

And if all goes well, you may never have to worry about job freezes and business slumps affecting workforces the world over.
